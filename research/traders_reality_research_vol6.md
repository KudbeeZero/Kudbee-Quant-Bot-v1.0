# SECTION 1: ICT LIQUIDITY FRAMEWORK — BSL/SSL, EQUAL HIGHS/LOWS, STOP HUNT ENGINEERING

## 1.1 BSL and SSL: Core Definitions

**Buy-Side Liquidity (BSL)** is the pool of resting buy-stop orders sitting *above* key price highs. These belong to short sellers who placed stops above highs to protect their positions. When price touches or exceeds those highs, those stops trigger as market buy orders — creating the exact liquidity institutions need to fill massive sell positions.

**Sell-Side Liquidity (SSL)** is the pool of resting sell-stop orders sitting *below* key price lows. These belong to long traders protecting their positions. When price dips below those lows, the stops trigger as market sell orders — providing liquidity for institutions to fill large buy orders.

**The mechanism:** Institutions cannot fill a 100,000-contract buy order at one price without a counterparty willing to sell 100,000 contracts. Retail stop clusters below lows *are* that counterparty. The "stop hunt" is simply how institutions source the volume they need.

### Where BSL Accumulates (Above)
- Previous swing highs (any timeframe)
- Equal highs (EQH) — highest-probability targets
- Previous day high (PDH), previous week high (PWH), previous month high (PMH)
- Session highs (Asian, London, NY)
- Round number psychological levels above structure
- Trendline resistance

### Where SSL Accumulates (Below)
- Previous swing lows (any timeframe)
- Equal lows (EQL)
- Previous day low (PDL), previous week low (PWL), previous month low (PML)
- Session lows
- Round number psychological levels below structure
- Trendline support

### Significance Ranking (Most to Least Significant)

1. **Untouched levels outrank touched levels.** Once a pool is swept, it is spent.
2. **Higher timeframe extremes outrank lower timeframe extremes.** Monthly high's BSL > 4H swing high's BSL.
3. **Older unconsumed levels carry more weight.** A swing high surviving 60 days without being swept has 60 days of accumulated stops.
4. **Confluence multiplies significance.** When PDH aligns with PWH at the same price zone, the combined pool is treated as a single mega-target.
5. **Equal highs/lows are the highest-probability single-timeframe target** — retail traders almost universally place stops just beyond obvious double-tops and double-bottoms.

## 1.2 Equal Highs (EQH) and Equal Lows (EQL)

### Exact Definition
An **Equal High (EQH)** forms when price makes a swing high, then on a subsequent swing returns to within a very tight range of that same high and rejects. **Equal Low (EQL)** is the mirror.

**The "equal" criterion:** Approximately **0.10%–0.20% of price** (TradingView community default: 0.15% tolerance). At $100,000 BTC, two highs within $150–$200 qualify as equal. The logic is behavioral — what matters is that retail traders *perceive* a double top/bottom and stack stops at the same level.

**Minimum touches:** Two touches form a valid EQH/EQL. Three or more touches create a higher-probability pool — more traders have now confirmed the level with stop placements.

### Why EQH/EQL Are the Highest-Probability Stop Hunt Targets

1. **Double tops/bottoms are the most widely-taught retail reversal pattern.** Every retail trader places a stop just above an obvious double top. Above an EQH: (a) short sellers' stops + (b) breakout buyers' entries = twice as dense as a normal swing high.
2. **Retail ALSO place breakout entries above double tops.** Both trigger as buy orders — the pool is doubly concentrated.
3. **Institutions know exactly where these orders are.** The levels are visible to every participant, making them algorithmic targets.
4. **Critical distinction:** A double top at equal highs does NOT confirm a reversal in ICT methodology — it confirms a BSL pool that will be **swept first**. The reversal, if it happens, comes *after* the sweep, not at it.

### Identification Rules by Timeframe

| Timeframe | EQH/EQL Type | Application |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly/Weekly | Macro targets | May take weeks to sweep |
| Daily | Primary pool for swing setups | Target of the week's directional move |
| 4H/1H | Intraday swing targets | Target for London or NY session move |
| 15M/5M | Scalp targets / execution-level triggers | Used for precise sweep confirmation |

## 1.4 Stop Hunt Engineering — The Five-Phase Market Maker Sequence

### Why MMs MUST Sweep Liquidity Before a Major Move

Institutions operating at scale cannot simply place a market order — it would spike the price before fill is complete. They need a willing seller of equivalent size. The only time that size is available is when retail stop orders are triggering en masse. The stop hunt *is* the fill mechanism.

**"There is no major move without a liquidity sweep first."** — ICT

### The Five-Phase Sequence

**Phase 1 — Original Consolidation (Range Building)**
Price oscillates within a defined range. BSL accumulates above range highs; SSL accumulates below range lows. Longer range = larger pool. This is the "charging" phase.

**Phase 2 — Expansion / False Move (Left Side)**
Price breaks in one direction, appearing to be a legitimate trend. Retail traders enter in the direction of the breakout. Creates the fuel for the reversal. Often involves multiple retracements with FVGs left behind.

**Phase 3 — Smart Money Reversal (The Turn)**
Price reaches a higher-timeframe PD array. Here: (a) it triggers the stop cluster at the extreme, (b) creates a strong displacement candle in the opposite direction, (c) leaves a Market Structure Shift (MSS) on the lower timeframe. This is the institutional entry point.

**Phase 4 — Distribution / Right Side**
Price retraces into previously formed Order Blocks and FVGs. Retail traders who missed the move now "confirm" the reversal and buy/sell into it. Institutions unload their Phase 3 positions into this retail demand.

**Phase 5 — Return to Consolidation**
Price completes the cycle by returning to the original range (Phase 1 origin) — the ultimate target of the entire structure.

## 1.5 Previous Day High/Low (PDH/PDL) — Complete Rules

### The Three PDH/PDL Scenarios

1. **Sweep and Reversal (most common, highest-probability trade):** Price spikes through PDH/PDL by 5–15 pips/points (enough to trigger stops), then immediately reverses back inside the prior day range. Classic fade setup.
2. **Breakout and Acceptance:** Price breaks PDH/PDL and multiple candles close and build structure beyond it. Do not fade — this is continuation.
3. **Rejection Without Sweep:** Price approaches but doesn't breach. Liquidity remains uncollected. Level remains a live target for the next session.

### Six-Step PDH/PDL Sweep and Reversal Entry Protocol

1. **Mark PDH and PDL** on the daily chart before the session opens
2. **Establish higher-timeframe bias** — is price in premium (selling zone) or discount (buying zone)?
3. **Wait for sweep confirmation** — a candle closes *beyond* PDH or PDL; preferably a close or decisive wick beyond
4. **Confirm reversal via CHoCH/MSS** on lower timeframe (15M or 5M) — break of a minor lower high (after PDH sweep) or minor higher low (after PDL sweep)
5. **Identify the FVG** created by the sweep displacement candle; this is the entry zone
6. **Enter at FVG 50% (Consequent Encroachment)** via limit order; or enter on the retest of the FVG with a rejection candle

**Stop placement:** Above the highest wick of the sweep candle (shorts from PDH sweeps); below the lowest wick of the sweep candle (longs from PDL sweeps).

**Targets:**
- Primary: Midpoint of the prior day's range
- Full: Opposing liquidity level (PDL for PDH sweeps; PDH for PDL sweeps)
- Minimum RR: 2:1 required before entry

**Best timing:** London Open (2:00–5:00 AM EST) and New York Open (7:00–10:00 AM EST). **Avoid PDH/PDL fade entries during Asian hours** — Asian session accumulates liquidity rather than sweeping it.

### Why PWH/PWL Are More Significant Than PDH/PDL

**Classic Tuesday High (Bearish week):**

**Wednesday Reversal Patterns:**

- Most common around NFP Fridays and in summer months (thin liquidity amplifies the sweep).

**Practical weekly rule:** Weekly high or low most often forms Tuesday or Wednesday (60–70% of weeks), with Monday being the manipulation/false-direction day and Thursday–Friday being distribution completion.

## 1.7 Liquidity Void vs. Liquidity Pool

These are the two fundamentally opposite ICT price delivery concepts:

| Aspect | Liquidity Pool | Liquidity Void |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Dense concentration of resting stop orders | Price range with no orders; area of rapid delivery |
| **Formation** | Accumulates at swing extremes over time | Created by explosive displacement candles |
| **Price behavior** | Price is drawn TOWARD it | Price is drawn BACK INTO it to fill |
| **ICT role** | Target of directional moves | Entry zone / pullback destination |
| **Visual signature** | Equal highs, swing highs/lows, prior session extremes | 3+ consecutive single-direction candles with no internal retracement |
| **Trading rule** | Fade when swept (reversal setup) | Enter on retest (continuation setup) |

### Liquidity Void Identification — 3-Step Process
1. **Identify explosive displacement:** 3+ consecutive candles in one direction with no internal overlap
2. **Mark the void:** Draw a zone from initiation to conclusion of displacement
3. **Wait for return:** Price will typically retrace back into this zone. The 50% midpoint (consequent encroachment) is the highest-probability entry within the void

**Key distinction from FVG:** A FVG is a 3-candle pattern. A liquidity void spans the entire multi-candle displacement and encompasses multiple FVGs stacked together. Every FVG is part of a liquidity void; not every liquidity void contains a standalone tradeable FVG.

## 1.8 Inducement (IDM) — Complete Rules

### Definition
**Inducement** is a deliberately engineered minor liquidity pool — a smaller, more obvious swing high or low — that price sweeps first to trap retail traders before making the true move to a larger, less obvious pool.

Internal pools = the "bait." External pools (previous swing extremes further away) = the true institutional target.

### How IDM Works (Bearish Example)

1. Price is in a downtrend. Pullback creates a short-term lower high. This lower high's BSL is the IDM level.
2. Retail traders who shorted the lower high have stops just above that minor swing high.
3. Price briefly sweeps above that IDM level (triggering stops), appears to reverse bullish, traps late long entries.
4. Price then aggressively reverses and sweeps the true target: the larger SSL pool below the prior major swing low.

**ICT rule:** "The first sweep of a session is often inducement. The second move is the real direction."

### Identifying Valid IDM Levels
- Forms as an **internal swing** within a larger trending structure
- Sits **between** the current price and the true higher-timeframe liquidity target
- Is the **most recently formed and most obvious** swing point
- Aligns with a BOS point from the prior smaller-timeframe move

### IDM → True Target Sequence
When price sweeps an IDM level and then creates an MSS without reaching the true pool, the IDM sweep becomes a high-conviction signal that the true pool is next.

**Entry signal:** Once IDM is swept and MSS is formed, enter in direction of the true pool with stop above/below the IDM sweep wick.

# SECTION 2: ICT POWER OF THREE (AMD) — COMPLETE INTRADAY FRAMEWORK

## 2.1 AMD Definition: The Three Phases

The ICT **Power of Three (PO3)**, also called the AMD model, describes how the Interbank Price Delivery Algorithm (IPDA) engineers every complete price move across all timeframes. Every candle — from 1-minute to yearly — is constructed through exactly three phases.

### Phase 1: Accumulation
Price consolidates in a tight horizontal range near the daily/session open. Smart money quietly builds positions. Volume is low. Candles are small-bodied and overlapping. **On the daily chart this is the Asian session.** The market "appears to be doing nothing" while liquidity stacks up above and below the range boundaries.

### Phase 2: Manipulation
An aggressive, fast, intentional move in the direction *opposite* to the true institutional bias. This is the **Judas Swing** — price sweeps accumulated stop orders on one side, triggers retail breakout entries in the wrong direction, and immediately reverses. Key characteristic: "aggressive displacement outside the range followed by a return back into it." The manipulation creates **the wick** of the daily candle.

### Phase 3: Distribution
The genuine, sustained institutional move in the true bias direction. Large-bodied candles. Expanding range. One-sided delivery with minimal pullback. Distribution forms **the body** of the daily candle.

### The Candle Encoding
- **Bullish day (OLHC candle):** Open → Low (manipulation/Judas Swing wick) → High → Close
- **Bearish day (OHLC candle):** Open → High (manipulation wick) → Low → Close

# SECTION 3: BINANCE AND BYBIT PERPETUAL FUTURES MECHANICS

## 3.1 Perpetual Contract Core — Three Critical Prices

| Price | Definition | Used For |
|---|---|---|
| **Index Price** | Weighted average of the asset's spot price across 10–16 major exchanges | Input to mark price; funding rate calculation |
| **Mark Price** | Derived fair value — median of three components | **Liquidation trigger; unrealized PnL** |
| **Last Price** | Most recent executed trade on the perpetual order book | Realized PnL only |

**Critical rule:** Liquidation triggers at **mark price** (not last price). A wick on the last price chart alone cannot liquidate you. Mark price incorporates a 30-second moving average of the mid-price relative to the index — in a real spike, mark price tracks last price closely with only slight lag. Practical rule: **Place stops where a genuine structural break occurs, not just where a brief last-price wick might reach.**

### Binance Mark Price Formula
```
Mark Price = Median(Price1, Price2, Contract Last Price)

Price1 = Index Price × [1 + Last Funding Rate × (Time Until Next Funding / Funding Period)]
Price2 = Index Price + Moving Average (30-second basis)
```

### Bybit Mark Price
- Same general formula structure
- Added a "TradFi Mark Price" clamp in November 2025: prevents mark price from deviating more than **3% from the index price** — better protection against mark price manipulation

## 3.2 Funding Rate Mechanics (Binance — Complete)

### The Formula (Post September 18, 2025 Update)
```
Funding Rate (F) = [Average Premium Index (P) + clamp(Interest Rate − P, −0.05%, +0.05%)] / (8 / N)

Where:
  N = funding interval in hours (8 for standard, 4 for selected pairs)
  Interest Rate = 0.01% per 8-hour interval
  clamp(x, min, max): constrains the value to the min/max range
```

### Settlement Times
Three times daily at **00:00 UTC, 08:00 UTC, and 16:00 UTC** (standard). Selected high-volatility pairs run 4-hour intervals.

**Critical rule:** You only pay/receive funding if you hold an open position **at the exact settlement timestamp.** Closing 1 second before avoids the payment entirely.

**Trading relevance:**
- **00:00 UTC settlement** → coincides with pre-London buildup window
- **08:00 UTC settlement** → coincides with London open (07:00 GMT) — compound effect of session open + funding settlement
- **16:00 UTC settlement** → falls at NY afternoon session

### Funding Rate Thresholds for Trading Decisions

| Funding Rate | Implication | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **> +0.10%/8h** (extreme positive) | Longs severely overcrowded; crash risk | High headwind for longs; potential short bias |
| **+0.05% to +0.10%** | Longs crowded | Caution on new longs; reduce size |
| **+0.01% to +0.05%** | Normal; slight long bias | No strong signal |
| **-0.01% to +0.01%** | Neutral | No signal |
| **-0.05% to -0.10%** | Shorts crowded | Squeeze potential; long bias |
| **< -0.10%** (extreme negative) | Shorts severely overcrowded; squeeze signal | High probability of short squeeze; long opportunities |

**Cap for BTC/USDT:** ±0.75 × Maintenance Margin Rate. At 0.4% MMR → cap = ±0.30% per 8 hours.

### Where to Find Predicted Funding Rate
- **On Binance UI:** Top of the futures chart, next to mark price display — shows current estimated rate and countdown timer
- **Dedicated page:** Binance Futures → Funding History → Real-Time Funding Rate
- **External:** CoinGlass (coinglass.com/FundingRate) aggregates both exchanges simultaneously
- **Leading indicator tip:** Bybit's funding rate (TWAP-weighted, more responsive) often leads Binance's funding rate. Monitor Bybit as an early warning system even if trading on Binance.

## 3.4 Leverage Levels and Liquidation Price Formulas

### Binance BTC/USDT Perpetual Leverage Tiers

| Position Notional (USDT) | Max Leverage | Maintenance Margin Rate |
|---|---|---|
| 0 – 50,000 | 125x | 0.40% |
| 50,001 – 250,000 | 100x | 0.50% |
| 250,001 – 1,000,000 | 50x | 1.00% |
| 1,000,001 – 10,000,000 | 20x | 2.50% |
| 10,000,001 – 20,000,000 | 10x | 5.00% |
| 20,000,001 – 50,000,000 | 5x | 10.0% |

**Bybit Tier 1 (BTC):** 0.50% MMR vs. 0.40% for Binance (slightly less favorable for large BTC positions).

### Exact Liquidation Price Formulas

**Isolated Margin, Long:**
```
Liquidation Price = Entry Price × [1 − (1/Leverage) + Maintenance Margin Rate]
Simplified: LP_long = Entry × (1 − 1/L + MMR)
```

**Isolated Margin, Short:**
```
Liquidation Price = Entry Price × [1 + (1/Leverage) − Maintenance Margin Rate]
Simplified: LP_short = Entry × (1 + 1/L − MMR)
```

|---|---|---|
| 125x | 0.40% | ~0.4% below entry |

| 50x | 1.00% | ~1.0% below entry |

### Why 10x Is Used for Session Trading
- **9–10% liquidation buffer** at small position sizes comfortably exceeds typical session stop distances (1–3%)
- Stop-loss fires on the chart **long before** liquidation
- Lower leverage means smaller funding cost per period
- At 10x: a 2% session stop = 20% loss on margin — the designed risk outcome
- Higher leverage (20x–125x) creates liquidation distances tighter than typical stop placements — a normal intraday move can liquidate you before your stop triggers

## 3.5 Position Sizing Formula — Exact Calculation

### Core Formula
```
Dollar Risk    = Account Balance × Risk%
Stop Distance  = |Entry Price − Stop Price| (in USDT)
Contract Qty   = Dollar Risk / Stop Distance  (in BTC)
Position Notional = Contract Qty × Entry Price
Required Margin    = Position Notional / Leverage
```

### Worked Examples (BTC = $100,000)

**$10,000 Account — 1% risk, 2% stop, 10x leverage:**
```
Dollar Risk = $100
Stop Distance = $2,000
Contract Qty = 0.050 BTC
Position Notional = $5,000
Required Margin = $500
```

**$50,000 Account — 1% risk, 1.5% stop, 10x leverage:**
```
Dollar Risk = $500
Stop Distance = $1,500
Contract Qty = 0.333 BTC
Position Notional = $33,300 (Tier 1: 0.4% MMR on Binance)
Required Margin = $3,330
Liquidation price (long) ≈ $90,400
```

**$100,000 Account — 1% risk, 2% stop, 10x leverage:**
```
Dollar Risk = $1,000
Stop Distance = $2,000
Contract Qty = 0.500 BTC
Position Notional = $50,000 (at Tier 1/Tier 2 boundary on Binance)
Required Margin = $5,000
```

### Key Sizing Rules
1. **Leverage is a margin efficiency tool, not a risk amplifier** — risk is set by the dollar risk formula; leverage only determines how much margin you post
2. **Always verify tier** — check if position notional crosses an MMR tier boundary; larger sizes increase MMR and bring liquidation price closer to entry
3. **Include funding cost in sizing** — at 0.01%/8h, a $100K notional position costs $10 per settlement. For a 4-session-hold (32 hours), add $40 to expected cost
4. **BTC/USDT contract minimum:** 0.001 BTC on Binance Futures

## 3.6 Cross-Exchange Basis Monitoring

### Definition
```
Basis = Perpetual Price − Spot Index Price
```

**Positive basis** (perp > spot) → positive funding rate → longs pay shorts → pressure to sell perp/buy spot → basis contracts.
**Negative basis** (perp < spot) → negative funding rate → shorts pay longs → pressure to buy perp/sell spot.

### Basis Signals for Session Direction

| Basis Signal | Implication |
|---|---|
| Strongly positive basis entering London open | Longs heavily crowded; elevated risk of stop-hunt/flush before continuation |
| Strongly negative basis before NY open | Shorts crowded; potential for short-squeeze on NY momentum |
| Basis rapidly expanding with rising price | New money entering via perpetuals — momentum likely genuine |
| Basis expanding against price direction | Spot buying without perpetual follow-through — divergence warning |
| Extreme positive funding (>0.05%) sustained 2+ periods | Historically precedes sharp corrections — headwind for long entries |

## 3.7 Order Types — What to Use When

| Order Type | Trigger | Execution | Best Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Limit** | Placed on book | At limit price or better | Planned entries at specific levels |
| **Market** | Immediate | Best available | Fast entry at session break, news reactions |
| **Stop-Market** | Mark or Last Price | Market order | **Hard stops — guaranteed exit, accepts slippage** |
| **Stop-Limit** | Mark or Last Price | Limit order | Stops where gap risk is low |
| **Trailing Stop** | Mark Price | Market order | Running profit on trending sessions |
| **OTOCO** (One-Cancels-Other) | Mark or Last Price | TP = Limit; SL = Stop-Market | Full position management in one order |

### Session Trading Order Hierarchy

**Entry:**
- *Limit* at confirmed level (preferred — saves maker fee, best fill)
- *Stop-Market* triggered on breakout (for breakout entries — faster but pays taker fee)

**Stop-Loss:**
- *Stop-Market triggered by Mark Price* — most reliable; cannot be faked by last-price wicks
- For high-liquidity sessions: *Stop-Limit* with $10–$20 limit buffer acceptable
- **Never use Last Price trigger for stops** unless specific reason — more susceptible to manipulation wicks

**Take-Profit:**
- *Limit order* at target — collects maker rebate, best fill
- *Trailing Stop* at 0.3–0.5% callback for momentum trades

**Recommended Full OTOCO Setup:**
```
Entry: Limit at level
TP: Limit at +R target
SL: Stop-Market at -R stop (Mark Price trigger)
→ If TP fills, SL auto-cancels
→ If SL fills, TP auto-cancels
```

## 3.8 Insurance Fund, ADL, and Liquidation Cascade

### How the Insurance Fund Works
- When a trader is liquidated at a price **better** than bankruptcy price → surplus goes **into** the insurance fund
- When liquidated at a price **worse** than bankruptcy price → **insurance fund covers** the deficit
- If insurance fund is insufficient → **ADL (Auto-Deleveraging)** is triggered

### ADL — Who Gets Auto-Deleveraged First
```
Ranking Score = PNL% × Effective Leverage (for profitable positions)
```

The most profitable, most highly leveraged winning positions on the opposite side get ADL'd first. A trader holding a 50x short that is 40% in profit would be near the top of the ADL queue.

### How to Avoid Being ADL'd
1. **Monitor the ADL indicator** in the futures UI (5-light bar per position; more lights = higher risk)
2. **Reduce effective leverage** — take partial profits; don't run very high PnL% at very high leverage simultaneously
3. **Use Isolated margin** instead of Cross
4. **Avoid holding through known high-cascade-risk periods** (post-major-news gaps, extreme funding settlements)

### Practical Signal — Insurance Fund Monitor
Monitor insurance fund size via CoinGlass. Rapid depletion during a volatility spike signals imminent ADL risk. Reduce position size or close profitable leveraged positions proactively when severe depletion is detected.

## 3.9 Slippage and Spread at Session Opens

### BTC/USDT Perpetual Spread Patterns

**Normal conditions:** ~$0.10–$1.00 bid-ask spread (~1 basis point at $100K BTC)

**Session-open conditions:**
- **London open (07:00–08:30 UTC):** Widened 2–5x normal spread briefly at the open. Also overlaps with 08:00 UTC funding settlement — compound effect. This is the highest-activity, highest-spread window.
- **NY open (13:00–14:30 UTC):** Similar widening; often less severe than London open unless macro news scheduled.
- **High-impact events (CPI, FOMC, NFP):** Spread can widen to $5–$50 for seconds during announcement. Market depth collapses momentarily.

**U-shaped intraday pattern:** Spreads peak immediately before and after each 8-hour funding settlement (06:00–08:30 UTC and 14:00–16:30 UTC), with a trough at mid-cycle.

### Practical Stop Placement Rules for Spread
1. **Add spread buffer to stops:** At London/NY open, add at least $10–$25 to stop distance to account for fill slippage on a stop-market order
2. **Use Stop-Limit for non-emergency exits:** Avoids market impact slippage; set limit $10–$50 below trigger for longs
3. **Use Stop-Market for news events:** Accept slippage cost for guaranteed execution
4. **Don't enter market orders at first candle of a session:** Wait 1–2 candles for depth to establish
5. **Funding settlement ±5 min is a no-trade zone** for precision entries

## 3.11 Session Trading Rules from Perpetual Mechanics

1. **Stop placement:** Use Mark Price trigger for all stop-loss orders. Add $15–$30 buffer to account for spread at session opens.
